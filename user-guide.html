1. Overview & Quick Start

This planner projects your retirement portfolio from today until your life expectancy. It models four account types, Social Security, a pension, employer matching, Roth conversions, and withdrawal taxes — all updated in real time as you change inputs.

Your data stays private. Everything is stored in your browser's local storage. Nothing is sent to any server.

Quick Start (5 minutes)

  1. 1Enter your birth year in Settings so the planner knows your current age.
  2. 2Go to Accounts and fill in current balances and monthly contributions for each account you own.
  3. 3Go to Income and add Social Security, pension, and employer match details.
  4. 4Go to Withdrawal Strategy and set your target retirement age and annual spending.
  5. 5Read the blue Portfolio Projection chart at the top — does your money last to your life expectancy?

2. Accounts Tab

Each account card has three inputs and a return selector. Changes update the projection immediately.

Traditional 401(k)

  • Pre-tax contributions; all withdrawals taxed as ordinary income
  • Employer match is added here (configured in the Income tab)
  • Subject to Required Minimum Distributions (RMDs) at age 73 or 75
  • At Retirement breakdown shows: your contributions / employer match / total growth

Roth 401(k)

  • After-tax contributions; qualified withdrawals are tax-free
  • Before age 59½, withdrawals follow the pro-rata rule — contributions and earnings come out proportionally
  • Set the Contribution Basis to track how much of your current balance is contributions (post-tax principal)

Roth IRA

  • After-tax contributions; qualified withdrawals tax-free
  • Contributions are always accessible penalty-free, regardless of age — the IRS lets you pull out what you put in first
  • Set the Contribution Basis to tell the planner how much of your balance is your actual contributions vs. growth
  • The At Retirement breakdown distinguishes contributions, mature conversions, pending conversions, and earnings

Brokerage (Taxable)

  • After-tax, no restrictions on withdrawals
  • No contribution limits; useful as a bridge account before age 59½
  • In the withdrawal strategy, brokerage is often drawn first to preserve tax-advantaged growth

Contribution Basis (Roth accounts only)

This field records the cost basis — the after-tax dollars you actually contributed, not counting gains. The planner uses this to apply IRS withdrawal ordering rules and warn you if early withdrawals would incur a 10% penalty.

Where to find your contribution basis: Log in to your brokerage or IRA custodian. Look for "Cost Basis," "Contribution History," or "Roth IRA Basis." If uncertain, set it conservatively (lower than actual) to get a conservative safety estimate.

At-Retirement Breakdown

Each account card shows a breakdown of its projected value at retirement, split into contributions, employer match (Traditional 401k only), and market growth. This helps you understand how much of your wealth is your own contributions vs. compounding returns.

Roth IRA penalty-free pool: The breakdown shows your penalty-free pool = contributions + conversions ≥ 5 years old. This is the amount you can access before age 59½ without penalty or tax.

3. Income Tab

Social Security

Toggle Social Security on and enter:

  • Monthly benefit — your estimated benefit at your chosen start age. You can find this on ssa.gov/myaccount.
  • Start age — when you begin collecting (see milestones section below for the tradeoffs).
The planner models Social Security taxation: up to 85% of your benefit is included in taxable income once combined income exceeds $34,000 (single) or $44,000 (MFJ). The remaining 15% is always tax-free.

Pension

If you have a defined-benefit pension, toggle it on and enter the monthly payment and the age it begins.

Employment Income & Employer Match

  • Annual Income — your gross salary. Used only to compute employer match; does not affect withdrawal projections directly.
  • Employer Match % — the percentage of your contribution the employer matches (e.g., 50 = 50 cents per dollar contributed).
  • Match Cap % of Salary — the salary percentage at which the match stops (e.g., 6 means the employer matches your contributions up to 6% of salary).

The planner shows a live preview of your annual match and warns you if you are leaving free money on the table by contributing below the match cap.

Example: Salary $100,000, match 100%, cap 6% of salary. If you contribute $500/month ($6,000/yr = 6% of salary), you get the full $6,000 employer match. If you only contribute $400/month (4.8% of salary), you receive only $4,800 — leaving $1,200 unclaimed.

4. Withdrawal Strategy Tab

Core Inputs

InputWhat It Does
Retirement AgeThe year contributions stop and withdrawals begin
Life ExpectancyProjection endpoint; set conservatively (age 90–95 is common)
Annual SpendingYour target annual expenses in today's dollars; inflated each year automatically
Inflation RateHow fast costs grow (default 2.5%). Applies to spending and tax brackets

Withdrawal Mode

Choose how spending scales over retirement:

  • Fixed Amount — you spend the same real (inflation-adjusted) dollar amount every year
  • Percentage of Portfolio — you spend a fixed percentage of your balance each year; spending adjusts automatically as your portfolio grows or shrinks. This prevents a zero-balance scenario but may mean spending cuts in bad markets.

Smart Withdrawal Order

The planner automatically determines the most tax-efficient order to draw from your accounts each year. The general logic is:

  1. Cover shortfall (spending minus income from SS and pension) by drawing from brokerage first — no tax hit on principal, and it preserves tax-advantaged growth.
  2. Then draw from Roth IRA — tax-free, no RMD, best to let it compound.
  3. Then draw from Roth 401(k).
  4. Finally draw from Traditional 401(k) — taxable, drawn last to minimize the tax bill.
Rule of 55: If you retire at age 55–59 and have separated from your employer, you can withdraw from your current employer's 401(k) without the 10% early penalty. The planner marks 401(k) withdrawals before age 59½ with a "10% Penalty" badge unless you qualify for this rule.

Early Roth IRA Withdrawal Warning

If your retirement age is below 59½ and your projected spending requires drawing beyond your penalty-free Roth IRA pool (contributions + conversions ≥ 5 years old), a red warning card appears showing:

  • Which ages are at risk
  • Your penalty-free pool at each age
  • The dollar amount at risk of triggering a 10% penalty
IRS Roth IRA withdrawal order: The IRS mandates you consume layers in this order:
  1. Contributions (your original after-tax deposits) — always penalty-free and tax-free
  2. Conversion principal ≥ 5 years old — penalty-free before 59½
  3. Conversion principal < 5 years old — 10% penalty before 59½
  4. Earnings — 10% penalty + income tax before 59½

5. Roth Conversion Tab

What Is a Roth Conversion Ladder?

A Roth conversion means moving money from a Traditional 401(k) (pre-tax) to a Roth IRA (after-tax) and paying the income tax now, at a potentially low rate, in exchange for tax-free growth and withdrawals later.

The ladder strategy involves doing systematic conversions each year during the period between retirement and when RMDs begin — typically the lowest-income window of your life.

Summary Metrics

  • Total Conv. Tax — total tax paid on conversions
  • RMD Balance Reduction — how much less your Traditional 401(k) has at RMD age compared to no conversions; a larger number means lower future RMDs and less forced income
Key insight: Paying $9,000 in tax now to avoid $15,000+ in RMD-driven taxes later is often a good trade. The planner helps you visualize exactly how much you save.

6. Monte Carlo Tab

Why Monte Carlo?

The main projection uses a single constant return rate — clean, but unrealistic. Real markets fluctuate. A sequence of poor returns early in retirement (called sequence-of-returns risk) can permanently deplete a portfolio even if the long-run average is fine.

Monte Carlo runs 1,000 simulations of your retirement, each with random annual returns drawn from a normal distribution centered on your assumed return rate. It answers: how often does your money last?

Success Rate

The headline number is the percentage of simulations where your portfolio survives to your life expectancy. General benchmarks:

Success RateInterpretation
95% or aboveVery safe; you could spend more or retire earlier
85%–95%Comfortable; within the range most planners target
75%–85%Acceptable; consider building a small buffer
Below 75%Risky; consider reducing spending, delaying retirement, or increasing savings

Fan Chart

The fan chart shows five percentile bands across all simulations:

  • Median (50th) — the middle outcome; half of simulations end better, half worse
  • 25th / 75th percentiles — the "likely range"
  • 10th / 90th percentiles — the extreme tails (unlucky and lucky market sequences)
Run Monte Carlo after finalizing your other inputs to get a realistic success probability. Re-run whenever you change spending, retirement age, or asset allocation.

10. Tax Concepts Explained

How the Planner Estimates Taxes

The planner uses 2025 federal income tax brackets, inflation-adjusted each year at your chosen inflation rate. It does not model state taxes, IRMAA (Medicare surcharges), ACA subsidy cliffs, or the Net Investment Income Tax (3.8%). These can matter — consult a tax advisor for a full picture.

Standard Deduction

The planner subtracts the standard deduction before applying tax brackets:

  • Single: $15,000 (2025)
  • Married Filing Jointly: $30,000 (2025)

This means for single filers, your first $15,000 of income in retirement is effectively tax-free. The bracket ceilings in the Roth Conversion table refer to taxable income (after deduction).

Social Security Taxation

The planner includes 85% of your Social Security benefit in the taxable income baseline when computing Roth conversion headroom and withdrawal taxes. This is the maximum inclusion rate, which applies when combined income (AGI + non-taxable interest + ½ of SS) exceeds $34,000 (single) or $44,000 (MFJ). The remaining 15% is always tax-free.

Required Minimum Distributions (RMDs)

Once you reach RMD age, the IRS requires annual withdrawals from Traditional accounts calculated by dividing your year-end balance by an IRS life-expectancy factor (roughly 25–27 at age 73, shrinking each year). The planner models this and adds RMD income to your taxable income estimate. Roth conversions before RMD age reduce your traditional balance and therefore your future RMD obligations.

11. Common Questions & Scenarios

My money runs out before my life expectancy — what can I do?

  1. Increase monthly contributions (earlier is better due to compounding)
  2. Delay retirement age by even 1–2 years (fewer withdrawal years + more accumulation)
  3. Reduce target annual spending in retirement
  4. Delay Social Security to 67 or 70 for a higher lifetime benefit
  5. Enable Roth conversions to reduce future RMD taxes
  6. Set withdrawal mode to "Percentage of Portfolio" — you never hit exactly $0 but spending adjusts with markets

Should I contribute to Traditional 401(k) or Roth 401(k)?

The classic rule: if your current tax rate is lower than your expected retirement rate, choose Roth (pay taxes now at the lower rate). If your current rate is higher, choose Traditional (defer taxes to retirement when you'll be in a lower bracket). Most people in their peak earning years favor Traditional; those early in their careers often favor Roth.

When should I start Social Security?

Delaying SS earns 8%/yr after FRA (age 67 for those born 1960+), up to age 70. The break-even age — where delayed benefits overtake early benefits in total — is typically around age 80–82. If you expect to live past 82, delaying usually pays off. The planner lets you model different start ages directly.

I plan to retire early (before 59½) — how do I avoid penalties?

Several strategies work together:

  • Brokerage account: no restrictions, draw from here first
  • Roth IRA contribution basis: you can always withdraw your original contributions (not earnings) without penalty
  • Roth conversion ladder: convert Traditional → Roth IRA starting 5 years before you need the money; those conversions become penalty-free after 5 years
  • Rule of 55: penalty-free 401(k) access if you left the employer in or after the year you turned 55
  • SEPP (72(t) payments): not modeled here; consult a tax advisor for this advanced strategy

What is the conversion 5-year clock and does it reset?

Each Roth conversion creates its own 5-year clock, separate from any other conversion. A conversion made at age 55 becomes accessible penalty-free at age 60. A conversion at age 57 becomes accessible at 62. The clocks do not reset — they run in parallel. Once you pass age 59½, all conversions are accessible regardless of age.

Why is the conversion amount different from what I'd expect?

The conversion fills your chosen bracket ceiling based on taxable income (after the standard deduction). The planner also accounts for income already "on the table" — pension, Social Security (85% included), and any other income. If you already have $20,000 of taxable income, the converter only adds enough to reach the bracket ceiling from $20,000, not from $0.

My employer match seems too low — is the calculation right?

The match is capped at two limits: the percentage of your contribution they match (e.g., 50%) and the cap as a percentage of salary (e.g., up to 6% of salary). The planner checks both and applies whichever is more restrictive. It also warns you if you're contributing below the cap threshold and leaving match money unclaimed.

Does the planner account for inflation?

Yes. The inflation rate (default 2.5%) is applied to:

  • Annual spending (your expenses grow each year)
  • Federal tax brackets and standard deduction (brackets are indexed to inflation)
  • Social Security benefit (SS includes a COLA; the planner inflation-adjusts SS income)

Account balances grow at their own return rate, independent of inflation.
